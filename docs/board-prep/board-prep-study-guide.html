Read this first — accuracy & how to use this guide

This is an unofficial study aid. Army regulations and doctrine change frequently. Every effort has been made to reflect the most current publications as of June 2026, with citations to the governing publication. Always confirm answers against the official publication on armypubs.army.mil and with your unit's board NCO / president of the board. Local boards may weight subjects differently and may use the board recorder's own answer key. Where a fact could not be fully verified it is marked “verify locally.”

How a board works

  • Appearance & bearing first. Reporting procedures, uniform, and military bearing are graded before you answer a single knowledge question.
  • Open-ended questions. Real boards are oral. Members ask a question and you answer aloud — so practice saying the answers, not just recognizing them. That is why this guide gives the open-ended Q&A first.
  • Know the source. Strong answers cite the publication (“Per AR 670-1…”). The multiple-choice sections reinforce the same facts for quick self-testing.
  • “Which regulation covers…?” Board members love regulation-identification questions. A dedicated section for these is at the front of this guide.

What's inside

Part I — Leadership & Doctrine · Part II — Personnel, Programs & Justice · Part III — Fitness & Health · Part IV — Soldier Skills & Warfighting. Each regulation section has: a currency header, open-ended Q&A, and multiple-choice questions with answers.

PART I · Leadership & Doctrine

ADP 6-22

Army Leadership and the Profession

Current edition: ADP 6-22, 31 July 2019. The Army's capstone leadership doctrine.

Open-ended Q&A

What does ADP 6-22 cover?
Army Leadership and the Profession — the principles of leadership and the Army profession.
Define leadership.
The activity of influencing people by providing purpose, direction, and motivation to accomplish the mission and improve the organization.
What are the three leader attributes (what a leader IS — “Be/Know”)?
Character, Presence, Intellect.
What are the three leader competencies (what a leader DOES)?
Leads, Develops, Achieves.
What makes up Character?
Army Values, empathy, the Warrior Ethos and service ethos, discipline, and humility.
What makes up Presence?
Military and professional bearing, fitness, confidence, and resilience.
What makes up Intellect?
Mental agility, sound judgment, innovation, interpersonal tact, and expertise.
What are the three levels of leadership?
Direct, Organizational, and Strategic.
What are the seven Army Values?
Loyalty, Duty, Respect, Selfless Service, Honor, Integrity, Personal Courage (LDRSHIP).
Recite the Warrior Ethos.
I will always place the mission first. I will never accept defeat. I will never quit. I will never leave a fallen comrade.
What are the two types of personal courage?
Physical courage and moral courage.
Under “Leads,” what are the competencies?
Leads others, builds trust, extends influence beyond the chain of command, leads by example, and communicates.
Under “Develops,” what are the competencies?
Creates a positive environment / fosters esprit de corps, prepares self, develops others, and stewards the profession.

Multiple choice

1. The three leader attributes are:
  1. Leads, Develops, Achieves
  2. Character, Presence, Intellect
  3. Loyalty, Duty, Respect
  4. Direct, Organizational, Strategic
B. Attributes = what a leader IS. (Leads/Develops/Achieves are the competencies.)
2. Which is a core leader competency?
  1. Presence
  2. Character
  3. Achieves
  4. Intellect
C — Achieves. The others are attributes.
3. How many Army Values are there?
  1. 5
  2. 6
  3. 7
  4. 8
C — 7 (LDRSHIP).
4. The three levels of leadership are direct, organizational, and:
  1. Tactical
  2. Strategic
  3. Operational
  4. Executive
B — Strategic.
FM 6-22

Developing Leaders (Leader Development)

FM 6-22, Leader Development. Companion to ADP 6-22; the “how-to” of developing leaders. Verify edition locally.

Open-ended Q&A

What does FM 6-22 cover?
Leader development — how leaders develop themselves and others.
What are the three domains of leader development?
Institutional, operational, and self-development.
What are the three pillars/means of developing a leader?
Education, training, and experience.
What are the fundamentals of leader development?
Set conditions for leadership; provide feedback, coaching, counseling, and mentoring; and create opportunities through challenging experiences.
What is mentorship?
The voluntary developmental relationship between a person of greater experience and one of lesser experience, characterized by mutual trust and respect.

Multiple choice

1. The three leader-development domains are institutional, self-development, and:
  1. Operational
  2. Strategic
  3. Generating
  4. Tactical
A — Operational.
2. Mentorship is best described as:
  1. A mandatory rated relationship
  2. A voluntary relationship built on mutual trust and respect
  3. Formal classroom instruction
  4. A type of counseling form
B. Mentorship is voluntary and future-focused.
ADP 6-0

Mission Command: Command and Control

Current edition: ADP 6-0, 31 July 2019.

Open-ended Q&A

What does ADP 6-0 cover?
Mission command — the Army's approach to command and control.
Define mission command.
The Army's approach to command and control that empowers subordinate decision making and decentralized execution appropriate to the situation.
What are the seven principles of mission command?
Competence, mutual trust, shared understanding, commander's intent, mission orders, disciplined initiative, and risk acceptance.
What is command and control?
The exercise of authority and direction by a properly designated commander over assigned and attached forces in the accomplishment of the mission.
What is commander's intent?
A clear and concise expression of the purpose of the operation and the desired military end state that supports mission command.
What is disciplined initiative?
Action in the absence of orders, when existing orders no longer fit, or when unforeseen opportunities or threats arise — consistent with the commander's intent.

Multiple choice

1. How many principles of mission command are there?
  1. 5
  2. 6
  3. 7
  4. 8
C — 7.
2. Which is NOT a principle of mission command?
  1. Mutual trust
  2. Mission orders
  3. Centralized execution
  4. Risk acceptance
C. Mission command emphasizes decentralized execution.
3. Mission command empowers subordinates through:
  1. Detailed step-by-step orders
  2. Mission orders and commander's intent
  3. Removing all risk
  4. Higher-headquarters approval of every decision
B.
ADP 3-0

Operations & the Warfighting Functions

ADP 3-0, Operations, 31 July 2019 (supersedes ADRP 3-0). See also FM 3-0, Operations (Oct 2022) for current tenets.

Open-ended Q&A

What are the six warfighting functions?
Command and control, movement and maneuver, intelligence, fires, sustainment, and protection. (“Mission command” was renamed command and control in 2019.)
What is a warfighting function?
A group of tasks and systems united by a common purpose that commanders use to accomplish missions and training objectives.
What are the four elements of decisive action?
Offense, defense, stability, and defense support of civil authorities (DSCA).
What are the eight elements of combat power?
The six warfighting functions plus the two dynamics of leadership and information.
What are the tenets of operations in FM 3-0 (2022)?
Agility, convergence, endurance, and depth.

Multiple choice

1. Which is one of the six warfighting functions?
  1. Leadership
  2. Information
  3. Sustainment
  4. Training
C — Sustainment. Leadership and information are the two dynamics of combat power, not warfighting functions.
2. The four elements of decisive action are offense, defense, stability, and:
  1. DSCA
  2. Deterrence
  3. Reconnaissance
  4. Mobilization
A — DSCA (Defense Support of Civil Authorities).
ADP 7-0

Training

ADP 7-0, Training, 31 July 2019.

Open-ended Q&A

What does ADP 7-0 cover?
Training units and developing leaders.
Who is responsible for training?
Commanders. NCOs train individuals, crews, and small teams.
What can a commander delegate?
Authority, but never responsibility.
What method does the unit training plan use?
Crawl–walk–run.
What is METL?
Mission-Essential Task List — the doctrinal framework of the tasks a unit must perform to accomplish its mission.
What are the three training domains?
Institutional, operational, and self-development.
Name key principles of training.
Train as you fight; train to standard; train to sustain; train to maintain; train to develop adaptability; commanders are responsible for training; NCOs train individuals, crews, and small teams. (Verify exact list against current ADP 7-0.)

Multiple choice

  1. Plan–prepare–execute
  2. Crawl–walk–run
  3. Assess–train–retest
  4. Top–down only
B — Crawl–walk–run.
2. A commander may delegate:
  1. Responsibility
  2. Authority
  3. Both
  4. Neither
B — Authority, but not responsibility.
3. NCOs are primarily responsible for training:
  1. Brigades
  2. Battalions
  3. Individuals, crews, and small teams
  4. Division staffs
C.
ADP 1

The Army

ADP 1, The Army, 31 July 2019.

Open-ended Q&A

What does ADP 1 cover?
The Army — its purpose, the profession, and how it serves the Nation.
What are the characteristics of the Army profession?
Trust, military expertise, honorable service, esprit de corps, and stewardship of the profession.
What components make up the Army?
One Regular Army and two reserve components — the Army National Guard and the Army Reserve (supported by Army Civilians). Established under Title 10, U.S. Code.
What is the difference between the operating force and the generating force?
The operating force consists of units organized, trained, and equipped to deploy and fight; the generating force mans, trains, equips, and ensures the readiness of all Army forces.
What is the Army's greatest strategic asset?
The all-volunteer force / its Soldiers.

Multiple choice

1. The Army consists of one Regular Army and how many reserve components?
  1. One
  2. Two
  3. Three
  4. Four
B — Two (Army National Guard and Army Reserve).
2. Which is a characteristic of the Army profession?
  1. Stewardship
  2. Centralization
  3. Bureaucracy
  4. Seniority
A — Stewardship (also trust, military expertise, honorable service, esprit de corps).
TC 7-22.7

The Noncommissioned Officer Guide

Current as of 14 Aug 2025. Covers NCO duties, responsibilities, authority, and NCO history.

Open-ended Q&A

What are the three types of duty?
Specified, directed, and implied.
What are the two basic responsibilities of every leader?
Accomplishment of the mission and the welfare of Soldiers.
What are the two types of authority?
Command authority and general military authority.
What is the NCO support channel?
The channel of communication and supervision that runs from the senior NCO (CSM) through subordinate NCOs; it parallels and reinforces the chain of command.
When was the U.S. Army established?
14 June 1775.
Who is considered the “Father of the NCO Corps”?
Baron Friedrich von Steuben, who wrote the “Blue Book” (Regulations for the Order and Discipline of the Troops of the United States, 1778).
What are the three ranks the NCO Corps is often described by?
The NCO is the “backbone of the Army.” Corporal (E-4) is the first NCO rank; the Sergeant Major of the Army is the senior enlisted Soldier.

Multiple choice

1. The three types of duty are specified, implied, and:
  1. Directed

  4. General
A — Directed.

  1. Specialist (E-4)
  2. Corporal (E-4)
  3. Sergeant (E-5)
  4. Private First Class (E-3)
B — Corporal (E-4). A Specialist is E-4 but is not an NCO.
3. The U.S. Army was established on:
  1. 4 July 1776
  2. 14 June 1775
  3. 11 November 1918
  4. 14 June 1777
B — 14 June 1775.
ATP 6-22.1

The Counseling Process

ATP 6-22.1, The Counseling Process. Counseling form: DA Form 4856.

Open-ended Q&A

Define counseling.
The process used by leaders to review with a subordinate the subordinate's demonstrated performance and potential.
What are the three approaches to counseling?
Directive, nondirective, and combined.
What are the three major categories of developmental counseling?
Event counseling, performance counseling, and professional growth counseling.
What are the four stages of the counseling process?
Identify the need for counseling; prepare for counseling; conduct the counseling session; follow up.
What are the qualities of an effective counselor?
Purpose, flexibility, respect, communication, and support.
What form is used to document counseling?
DA Form 4856, Developmental Counseling Form.
Give examples of event counseling.
Reception & integration, crisis, referral, promotion, transition, adverse separation, and superior/substandard performance counseling.

PART II · Personnel, Programs & Justice

AR 670-1 / DA PAM 670-1

Wear and Appearance of Army Uniforms and Insignia

AR 670-1 current as of 2025; grooming/appearance standards substantially revised by the Army directive signed 12 September 2025 (Soldiers had 30 days to comply). Always confirm the latest directive.

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 670-1 cover?
The wear and appearance of Army uniforms and insignia. DA PAM 670-1 provides the detailed “how-to” and illustrations.
(2025 update) What are the current standards for women's ponytails/braids?
No wider than two inches and must hang within six inches from the top of the collar; ponytails are authorized only in the ACU and physical fitness uniform — not in dress uniforms.
(2025 update) What are the current female nail standards?
Clear polish only; length limited to ¼ inch, in a square or rounded shape.
(2025 update) What are the female cosmetic/lip standards?
No lipstick or colored nail polish; only clear or skin-toned lip gloss is authorized.
(2025 update) What are the female earring rules?
One per ear — gold, silver, diamond, or pearl studs — authorized with dress and combat uniforms.
(2025 update) What are the male hair standards?
Tapered or faded, evenly blended around the sides and back; maximum about two inches on top and one inch on the sides and back.
(2025 update) May male Soldiers wear nail polish?
No. Male Soldiers may not wear nail polish; nails are neatly trimmed and not past the fingertip.
Who is responsible for ensuring Soldiers maintain uniform/appearance standards?
Commanders and leaders at all levels — but ultimately every Soldier is responsible for their own appearance.

C. Not authorized with dress uniforms.

AR 600-8-19

Enlisted Promotions and Reductions

Current edition: 21 June 2024 (effective 21 July 2024); administrative revision 17 March 2025.

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 600-8-19 cover?
Enlisted promotions and reductions.
Which promotions are semi-centralized vs. centralized?
SGT (E-5) and SSG (E-6) are semi-centralized (point-based); SFC (E-7) through SGM (E-9) are centralized (HQDA board).
What is the maximum number of promotion points for SGT and SSG?
800 points total for each grade.
What is the maximum for civilian education?
135 points for SGT and 160 points for SSG.
What is the primary zone vs. secondary zone?
The primary zone is normal eligibility; the secondary zone allows early (accelerated) consideration for high-performing Soldiers.
Who has promotion authority for SGT/SSG?
The battalion (or equivalent) commander, per the HQDA point cutoff.

Multiple choice

1. Promotions to SGT and SSG are:
  1. Centralized (HQDA board)
  2. Semi-centralized (point-based)
  3. Automatic
  4. Decentralized to the squad
B. Semi-centralized via the promotion-point system.
2. The maximum promotion points for SGT/SSG is:
  1. 600
  2. 700
  3. 800
  4. 1000
C — 800.
AR 600-8-2

Suspension of Favorable Personnel Actions (Flags)

AR 600-8-2. Flag is initiated/removed on DA Form 268.

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 600-8-2 cover?
Suspension of favorable personnel actions — commonly called a “flag.”
What form is used to flag a Soldier?
DA Form 268.
What favorable actions does a flag stop?
Promotion, awards, schooling, reassignment (PCS), reenlistment/extension, favorable separation, and similar favorable actions.
What is the difference between a transferable and nontransferable flag?
A transferable flag (e.g., commander's investigation) moves with the Soldier on PCS; a nontransferable flag (e.g., APFT/AFT failure, adverse action) generally must be resolved before transfer.
How soon must a flag be reviewed?
Flags are reviewed periodically (monthly) and removed within the prescribed time after the circumstance is resolved. Verify current timelines.

Multiple choice

  1. DA Form 4856
  2. DA Form 268
  3. DA Form 705
  4. DA Form 638
B — DA Form 268.
2. Which favorable action does a flag suspend?
  1. Promotion
  2. Pay
  3. Meal card
  4. Sick call
A — Promotion (and awards, schooling, PCS, reenlistment, etc.).

AR 623-3 / DA PAM 623-3

Evaluation Reporting System (NCOER / OER)

AR 623-3, Evaluation Reporting System; procedures in DA PAM 623-3. NCOER uses the DA Form 2166-9 series.

What does AR 623-3 cover?
The Army's Evaluation Reporting System — NCOERs and OERs.
What is the purpose of the NCOER?
To document an NCO's performance and potential, strengthen the NCO corps, and provide selection boards/HRC data for personnel decisions.
What forms make up the NCOER?

Who is in the NCOER rating chain?
The rater, the senior rater, and the reviewer (and the rated NCO).
What is a rating scheme/published rating chain?
The documented designation of who rates, senior rates, and reviews each Soldier.

AR 600-20

Army Command Policy (Chain of Command, EO, SHARP)

AR 600-20, Army Command Policy. Includes the chain of command/NCO support channel, Equal Opportunity, and SHARP.

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 600-20 cover?
Army Command Policy — command authority, the chain of command and NCO support channel, the Equal Opportunity (EO) program, SHARP, and prohibited conduct (hazing, bullying, extremism).
What is the chain of command?
The succession of commanders, superior to subordinate, through which command is exercised — the “backbone” of military authority.
What is the NCO support channel?
The channel of supervision from the CSM through the chain of NCOs that parallels and reinforces the chain of command.
What does SHARP stand for?
Sexual Harassment/Assault Response and Prevention.
What are the two types of sexual-assault report?
Restricted (confidential, no command/law-enforcement notification) and Unrestricted (triggers investigation and command notification).
What is the purpose of the EO program?
To provide equal opportunity and fair treatment for military personnel and Family members without regard to race, color, religion, gender, or national origin, and to create a climate free of unlawful discrimination.
Define hazing and bullying.
Hazing is conduct that subjects a Soldier to abuse/humiliation as a rite of passage; bullying is aggressive behavior intended to harm or intimidate. Both are prohibited.

AR 600-25

Salutes, Honors, and Visits of Courtesy (Customs & Courtesies)

AR 600-25, Salutes, Honors, and Visits of Courtesy.

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 600-25 cover?
Military customs and courtesies — salutes, honors, and visits of courtesy.
What is the difference between a custom and a courtesy?
A custom is an established practice (tradition); a courtesy is an act of politeness/respect (e.g., the salute).
Whom do you salute?
Commissioned and warrant officers, the President, and recipients of the Medal of Honor; also render honors to the U.S. flag and national anthem.
When do you NOT salute?
Indoors (unless reporting), when in formation (only on command), when carrying articles with both hands, in combat/tactical conditions, or when it is impractical/unsafe.
What do you do during Reveille and Retreat?
Render honors to the flag — come to attention and salute (when in uniform and outdoors) during the national anthem/“To the Color.”
What are the three general orders... (commonly tested)?
1) I will guard everything within the limits of my post and quit my post only when properly relieved. 2) I will obey my special orders and perform all my duties in a military manner. 3) I will report violations of my special orders, emergencies, and anything not covered in my instructions to the commander of the relief.

  4. When meeting your commander outside

AR 27-10

Military Justice

AR 27-10, Military Justice (administers the UCMJ).

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 27-10 cover?
The administration of military justice under the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ).
What is an Article 15?
Nonjudicial punishment (NJP) — a commander's tool to discipline Soldiers for minor offenses without a court-martial.
What are the three types of court-martial?
Summary, Special, and General.
What are the categories/levels of Article 15?
Summarized, Company-grade (field-grade in some contexts), and Field-grade — differing in the maximum punishment authorized.
May a Soldier refuse an Article 15?
Yes (except aboard a vessel) — the Soldier may demand trial by court-martial instead.
What does UCMJ stand for?
Uniform Code of Military Justice.

PART III · Fitness & Health

FM 7-22 · AFT

Holistic Health and Fitness (H2F) & the Army Fitness Test

FM 7-22, Holistic Health and Fitness (Oct 2020) — replaced “Army Physical Readiness Training.” The Army Fitness Test (AFT) replaced the ACFT on 1 June 2025.

Open-ended Q&A

What does FM 7-22 cover?
Holistic Health and Fitness (H2F) — the Army's system for building and sustaining Soldier readiness. (Note: older guides incorrectly say “Physical Readiness Training” — that is outdated.)
What are the five domains of H2F?
Physical, nutritional, mental, spiritual, and sleep readiness.
What test replaced the ACFT, and when?
The Army Fitness Test (AFT), effective 1 June 2025.
What are the five AFT events?
3-Repetition Maximum Deadlift (MDL), Hand-Release Push-Up (HRP), Sprint-Drag-Carry (SDC), Plank (PLK), and the 2-Mile Run (2MR).
What event was removed from the old ACFT to create the AFT?
The Standing Power Throw (the medicine-ball throw).
What are the AFT scoring standards?
Combat MOS: one sex-neutral, age-normed standard requiring a total of 350 with a minimum of 60 per event. Combat-enabling MOS: sex- and age-normed, requiring a total of 300.
How many points is each AFT event worth?
Each event is scored 0–100, for a maximum of 500 points (five events).
What are the three fundamental components of physical readiness?
Strength, endurance, and mobility.

AR 600-9

The Army Body Composition Program (ABCP)

AR 600-9. Standards updated 2023–2026: one-site (abdominal) circumference tape and a Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR) standard. Confirm the current ALARACT/directive.

Open-ended Q&A

What does AR 600-9 cover?
The Army Body Composition Program — height/weight screening and body-fat standards.
What circumference method does the Army currently use?
A one-site abdominal (waist) circumference tape method for both men and women (adopted 9 June 2023; sole method since 2024).
Where and how is the abdominal measurement taken?
Against the skin at the navel, parallel to the floor, at the end of a normal relaxed exhalation; three measurements are averaged.
What is the new Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR) standard?
Waist circumference (at the navel) divided by height must be less than 0.55. (Per 2025–2026 directives — verify locally.)
Who is exempt from the tape/body-fat assessment?
Soldiers who achieve a high fitness-test score per the current directive (historically 540+ with 80+ per event on the record ACFT) — confirm the current AFT-based exemption threshold.
How often are Soldiers assessed?
Active component at least every 6 months; Reserve/Guard annually or as required.

TC 4-02.1

First Aid

TC 4-02.1, First Aid (aligns with Tactical Combat Casualty Care — TCCC).

Open-ended Q&A

What does TC 4-02.1 cover?
First aid — the emergency care a Soldier gives to prevent death or further injury.
What are the three phases of Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC)?
Care Under Fire, Tactical Field Care, and Tactical Evacuation Care.
What does the MARCH acronym stand for?
Massive hemorrhage, Airway, Respiration, Circulation, Head injury/Hypothermia.
What is the #1 cause of preventable death on the battlefield?
Massive hemorrhage (severe bleeding from an extremity) — treated first, often with a tourniquet.
What are the four life-saving steps / priorities of first aid?
Open the airway/restore breathing, stop the bleeding, prevent/treat for shock, and dress/protect the wound.
How long can a tourniquet generally be left on, and what must you do?
Apply tight enough to stop bleeding, mark the time (“T” and time), and never cover it; reassessment/conversion is done by medical personnel.

TC 4-02.3

Field Hygiene and Sanitation

TC 4-02.3, Field Hygiene and Sanitation.

Open-ended Q&A

What does TC 4-02.3 cover?
Field hygiene and sanitation — preventive medicine measures (PMM) that keep Soldiers healthy and the force combat-ready.
What is the leading cause of casualties historically — combat or disease/non-battle injury?
Disease and non-battle injury (DNBI) — which is why field sanitation matters.
What is the Field Sanitation Team (FST)?
A unit-level team (minimum two trained members) that advises the commander on preventive medicine measures.
What are the five preventive medicine measures (individual)?
Practice personal hygiene; protect against heat/cold injuries; protect against arthropods/insects (use repellent, treat uniforms); purify/drink safe water; avoid foodborne illness.
What is the chain of disease transmission concept?
Break the chain — agent, reservoir, and means of transmission — through PMM to prevent illness.

PART IV · Soldier Skills & Warfighting

TC 3-21.5

Drill and Ceremonies

TC 3-21.5, Drill and Ceremonies.

Open-ended Q&A

What does TC 3-21.5 cover?
Drill and ceremonies — the movements and procedures for moving and presenting units.
What are the two parts of a drill command?
The preparatory command (e.g., “Forward”) and the command of execution (e.g., “MARCH”).
What is the difference between a two-part command and a combined command?
A two-part command has a preparatory command and a command of execution; a combined command has them in one (e.g., “FALL IN,” “AT EASE”).
What is cadence?
The uniform step and rhythm of marching — quick time is 120 steps per minute; double time is 180 steps per minute.
What is the length of a 30-inch step at quick time?
The standard marching step is 30 inches at 120 steps per minute (quick time).
What is the difference between “At Ease” and “Rest”?
At Ease — keep the right foot in place, may move/relax but remain silent; Rest — keep the right foot in place but may talk.

Map reading and land navigation.
What are the five major terrain features?
Hill, ridge, valley, saddle, and depression.
What are the three minor terrain features?
Draw, spur, and cliff.

Cut and fill.
What are the five basic colors on a military map and what do they represent?
Black (man-made), Blue (water), Green (vegetation), Brown (contours/relief), Red (major roads/boundaries). (Red-brown on some maps.)
What are the three norths?
True north, magnetic north, and grid north.
How do you read a grid coordinate?
Read right (easting), then up (northing) — “right and up.”
What is a contour line?
A line on a map joining points of equal elevation; the three types are index, intermediate, and supplementary.

TC 3-22.9

Rifle and Carbine (Weapons — M4/M16)

TC 3-22.9, Rifle and Carbine.

Open-ended Q&A

What does TC 3-22.9 cover?
The rifle and carbine (M4/M16 family) — employment, marksmanship, and maintenance.
What is the maximum effective range of the M4 carbine against a point target?
Approximately 500 meters (point target); about 600 meters for an area target.
What are the four fundamentals of marksmanship?
Steady position, aim (sight alignment/sight picture), breathing (control), and trigger squeeze.
What is the cycle of function of the rifle (8 steps)?
Feeding, chambering, locking, firing, unlocking, extracting, ejecting, and cocking.
What does SPORTS stand for (immediate action)?
Slap, Pull, Observe, Release, Tap, Squeeze (Shoot).
What is the cyclic rate of fire of the M4?
Approximately 700–970 rounds per minute (cyclic); sustained rate is much lower (~12–15 rpm).
What are the weapons safety rules?
Treat every weapon as if loaded; never point at anything you don't intend to engage; keep your finger off the trigger until ready to fire; keep the weapon on safe until you intend to fire.

Chemical, Biological, Radiological, and Nuclear.
What are the MOPP levels?

What report is used to give first warning of a CBRN attack?
The CBRN 1 Report (NBC-1).

Code of Conduct · FM 6-27

Code of Conduct & the Law of Land Warfare

Six.
Recite Article I of the Code of Conduct.

“I will never surrender of my own free will. If in command, I will never surrender the members of my command while they still have the means to resist.”

Search, Silence, Segregate, Safeguard, Speed to the rear, and Tag.

Military necessity, distinction (discrimination), proportionality, humanity (avoiding unnecessary suffering), and honor.
